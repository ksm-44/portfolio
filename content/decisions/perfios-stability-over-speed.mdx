title: "Designed for stability over speed in a fintech HITL flow"
date: "2025-08-20"
status: "validated"
tldr: "Keeping a human on the risky decisions was slower — and it was exactly what made institutions comfortable enough to adopt."
---

## Hypothesis

In a document-intelligence flow for banks and lenders, more automation would mean more value: take the human out of as many steps as possible.

## Evidence

The users are financial institutions, where a confidently wrong extraction is worse than no extraction, and where trust is a precondition for adoption, not a byproduct of it. Removing human checkpoints to chase speed would have undercut the accuracy and auditability these buyers weigh above everything else.

## Result

I kept humans on the high-risk decisions and let automation carry the volume. Manual review effort for credit-operations teams still dropped by about half, and the conservative design is part of why use cases moved from demo to production at all.

## What I'd do differently

I'd set the "which decisions must stay human" line explicitly at the start of each use case, rather than discovering it mid-build for a few of them.
