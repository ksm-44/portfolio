## Why this problem

I built an India Portfolio Analyzer as a personal tool and saw the pattern first-hand: analysis is easy to generate, but "so what do I do now" is where retail investors get stuck. There's a genuinely interesting constraint that makes this a real product problem, not a feature request: SEBI regulation. Only registered advisers can give personalised, accountable advice; everyone else must stay general and educational. So the challenge is making analysis feel actionable without crossing into regulated advice you're not licensed to give.

## Real competitive landscape

**INDmoney** aggregates MFs, stocks, US equities, and shows where you deviate from targets — but it explicitly isn't a SEBI-registered adviser, so its suggestions stay general. **Dezerv's Wealth Monitor** benchmarks each fund's XIRR and assigns statuses, while its business monetises via managed portfolios — strong at diagnosis and steering toward its own products. **Kuvera** and **Groww** cover direct plans. The consistent theme: everyone is good at flagging; the honest, conflict-free "what should I do, and can I trust why" layer is thin — partly because regulation makes it hard, partly because several players' business models create advice conflicts.

## The gap, as a hypothesis

Retail investors don't primarily lack analysis — they lack a trustworthy, non-conflicted bridge from a flag to a decision they understand well enough to act on. The winning move isn't more diagnostics; it's decision scaffolding: explain a flag in plain language, lay out the options (not a directive), show the trade-offs, and be transparent that it isn't personalised advice — turning the regulatory constraint into a trust feature.

## Options and recommendation

**A — better diagnostics:** adds to the pile everyone has. **B — decision-scaffolding layer:** plain-language explanation, 2–3 option paths with trade-offs, explicit "not personalised advice" framing. **C — RIA-marketplace bridge:** stay educational, then hand off to fee-only registered advisers for accountable advice. I'd build **B as the core, with C as the escape hatch**. I'd reject anything that dresses up general suggestions as personalised advice — that's both a regulatory risk and exactly the conflict users distrust. Trade-off: B is slower and demands real content and UX craft, and its ceiling depends on how much value is legally deliverable without an RIA licence.

## What I'd validate and measure

8–10 interviews with the self-directed ₹5–50L segment on where they get stuck between seeing a flag and acting; a recurring-flags analysis from my own portfolio-analyzer outputs; and regulatory scoping on permissible educational framing. North star: the share of flagged issues where the user takes a considered next step — an action metric, not a vanity view. Guardrail: zero complaints of misleading, advice-like framing.
