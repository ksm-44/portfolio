## Problem

Thousands of expense trackers exist and almost everyone abandons them within
weeks. Every tracker makes the same demand: open an app, tap through five
screens, pick a category, type an amount. That's a minute of effort, eight to
ten times a day. People skip one entry, the balance stops matching reality,
trust collapses, the app gets deleted.

Auto-import apps fix the effort but fail the Indian context: they miss cash and
UPI-to-friend flows, can't represent Splitwise-style shared expenses, and
require handing complete financial history to a third party.

The job was never "track my expenses." It was "capture what I just spent, in
the two seconds I'm willing to give it, without trusting anyone else with my data."

## Constraints

One person, two weeks, zero budget. No bank API partnerships available to an
individual. Had to run on free-tier compute and a free LLM tier or the ₹0/month
positioning dies.

## Decision

<DecisionPoint
  question="What's the capture mechanism for a tracker that must not be abandoned?"
  options={[
    "Auto-import (SMS parsing + spending-app integrations) — zero user effort",
    "A native mobile app with a fast entry form — better UX than incumbents",
    "A Telegram bot with an LLM parser — text it the way you'd text a friend",
  ]}
  actual={2}
>
  Auto-import was my original plan, but each integration is slow to build,
  breaks silently when formats change, and still misses cash and split
  expenses — the exact transactions that make balances drift. Error-prone
  automation is worse than none, because it destroys trust in the numbers. A
  native app is still a new app to open and a new habit to form, and habit
  formation is the whole failure mode. So: users text "Zomato 300 from BOB" the
  way they'd text a friend. No new app, no new habit; the LLM handles 11 intent
  types including splits and corrections. Capture became fast enough that cash
  and split expenses — the ones every tracker misses — actually get logged.
</DecisionPoint>

The second structural call: a double-entry ledger instead of a list of
expenses. Every transaction posts entries against accounts; balances are the
sum of entries, so transfers, splits, and card payments balance by
construction. Trust in the numbers is the retention mechanic, so correctness
was architected in, not patched on.

Privacy works the same way — through ownership, not policy. The entire
financial history lives in a single SQLite file on the user's own free-tier
server, with nightly backups to their own Google Drive. There is no company
server to trust.

## Outcome

In daily use as my primary money system: every rupee across 6 accounts (banks,
cash, credit card, Splitwise) flows through it. 1,000+ transactions in 2
months. Zero balance drift by design. ~2,600 lines of Python from zero to
production in two weeks, plus a self-hosting replication guide.

What went wrong: I spent early effort scoping the auto-import path before
admitting it was a dead end for a solo builder. That was time I could have
spent on the parser. The insights engine also shipped later than it should
have — for the first weeks the product captured well but told me nothing.

## Learning

Friction, not features, kills this category. The winning move was instrumenting
a behavior people already have — narrating spending in chat — instead of
demanding a new one.
