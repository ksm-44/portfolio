## Problem

During a World Cup, a moment goes stale in about 30 minutes. A solo creator
can't watch 104 matches, pull stats, design a post in team colours, and publish
repeatedly for 39 days; media houses do it with newsrooms of 20+. And "just use
ChatGPT" produces what floods every feed: same-voiced, occasionally
hallucinated posts that platforms downrank.

The real problem: give one person a newsroom's coverage capacity without
sacrificing editorial voice or factual accountability.

## Constraints

16GB of total memory on one Mac — the LLM (~6GB), image generation (~6GB), and
video rendering can't coexist. 100 free API calls/day against 104 matches. Live
positional data is a paid enterprise product I couldn't afford. And AI content
can't be fully trusted, so publishing couldn't be autonomous.

Each constraint forced a decision: a job queue instead of a swarm (one heavy
process resident at a time), offline overnight art batches with millisecond
compositing on the hot path, cached responses with explicit
coverage-prioritization scoring, and two honest animation modes —
clearly-labelled stylized recreations from coarse live data, full-fidelity
showcases from free historical data. Scoping the truthful version instead of
overpromising is the decision I'm proudest of in this build.

## Decision

<DecisionPoint
  question="The pipeline can render and post autonomously. Do you let it?"
  options={[
    "Full auto-publish — maximum speed inside the 30-minute virality window",
    "Auto-publish with spot checks — faster, mostly safe",
    "Mandatory human gate — every render needs explicit approval",
  ]}
  actual={2}
>
  For a brand built on factual receipts, one hallucinated stat costs more
  credibility than 50 posts earn — and with spot checks, "mostly" is the
  problem, because the failure mode is silent and public. So every render lands
  on my phone via Telegram with Approve / Reject / Revise buttons; Revise feeds
  notes back into regeneration. Architecturally there is no path to publish
  without approval. Zero unreviewed posts went out across the whole tournament,
  and keeping platform uploads manual in v1 also cut a 2–4 week app-review
  dependency off the critical path.
</DecisionPoint>

Distribution did more work than the model: an original villain character, "El
Víbora," who attacks hype with contradicting stats — governed by a safety
policy written before launch, not after an incident: stats and tactics only,
never identity, nationality, or appearance. Public pre-match predictions
revisited post-match, win or lose, because accountability compounds trust in a
way hot takes can't. And an IPL-crossover content line as an Indian-market
wedge no global page could copy.

## Outcome

One person sustained newsroom-scale coverage for 39 straight days at ~$0
marginal cost. The 16GB constraint produced an architecture that was cheaper
and more reliable than the cloud version would have been.

What went wrong, honestly: I never instrumented the approve/reject funnel, so I
have no approval-rate number and no labelled dataset for prompt tuning — a
self-inflicted analytics gap that a PM of all people should not have shipped.
And I built the IPL-crossover pipeline before validating demand with even 10
manual posts; I automated ahead of the demand signal.

## Learning

Constraints shape better products than budgets. Every hard limit — memory, API
quota, missing data — forced a decision that made the system cheaper, more
honest, or more reliable. The AI was the least interesting component, which is
the point.
