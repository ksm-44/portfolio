## Problem

Families run their lives across a dozen apps that don't talk to each other: one
calendar for school, another for activities, bills in one app, receipts in an
inbox, medical records in a portal, warranties in a drawer. The cost is
concrete: missed renewals, expired warranties, subscriptions nobody uses, and
hours of weekly coordination work that lands unevenly on one family member.

I wrote a full business plan and PRD before building anything, because the risk
here was never "can this be built." It was "will anyone change their habits for it."

## Constraints

Solo, self-funded, evenings and weekends. The business plan budgets a 6-month
MVP with a $454K team on paper; the real constraint was that I had neither, so
validation had to come before code. No user base to test against, so the
waitlist landing page had to serve as the demand signal.

## Decision

The hard call was the pricing model. The plan I first wrote assumed families
would pay a monthly subscription, framed around ~$12/mo ARPU. My actual target
market was Indian households. When I checked that assumption against how Indian
consumers actually behave, it fell apart: Indian users overwhelmingly don't pay
app subscriptions.

<DecisionPoint
  question="FamilyOS needs a revenue model for the Indian household market. Which one?"
  options={[
    "Subscription (~₹300–₹800/mo) — clean, predictable, aligned with the US playbook",
    "One-time purchase — removes recurring friction",
    "Free for families, revenue from B2B/ads partners at moments of intent",
  ]}
  actual={2}
>
  Indian subscription conversion for utility apps is notoriously poor; I'd be
  pricing out my own market. A one-time purchase removes friction but caps LTV
  on exactly the users who engage most, with no recurring revenue to fund an
  ongoing platform. Going free-for-families meant rewriting the revenue section
  of the plan — which also changed the P0 roadmap: features that create
  partner-relevant moments of intent (renewals, warranties, document expiry)
  moved up.
</DecisionPoint>

## Outcome

Live waitlist site with 60+ signups and no paid acquisition.

The roadmap and
business plan are complete; the app build is in progress.

What went wrong: I wrote the entire first business plan US-framed — US
household counts, dollar ARPU, US competitor set — while my actual target was
Indian families. The numbers made the TAM look great and told me nothing about
my real market. I had to redo the monetization thinking from scratch. The plan
now keeps the global sizing for context, but the operating assumptions are
Indian-market-first.

## Learning

Building for Indian families is a different discipline from any B2B platform
I've worked on. Enterprise users tolerate friction if the tool saves their team
money; families abandon anything that demands new habits, and they don't pay
upfront. Validate the business model against the market you're actually in, not
the market your template was written for.
