## Why this problem

The household-organisation category is real, funded, and maturing — but it's built around the Western nuclear family: two parents, kids, a shared calendar, chores. India's coordination problem is structurally different: elders, extended family, domestic help, festival and ritual logistics, cash-and-informal errands. I have a genuine reason to care because I've been building in this space, which is a strength only if I'm honest about what's a thesis versus what's validated.

## Real competitive landscape

**Cozi** is the long-time default (shared calendar, lists, meal planning); in 2024 it restricted free users to a 30-day calendar window, pushing paid tiers, and reviewers note a dated interface. **Maple** is a newer all-in-one hub with a more generous free tier and AI throughout, positioned as the switch for people leaving Cozi. **Skylight** is hardware-first: a wall display with chores and calendar sync. A recurring insight across reviews: the app isn't the hard part, the habit is — the families who succeed put everything into one system. That's a retention and behaviour problem, which is squarely product.

## The gap, as a thesis

None of the leaders are designed for the Indian joint/multi-generational household. I'd expect unmet needs around coordinating three generations (including elders who may not use apps), managing domestic-help schedules and payments, and festival/extended-family logistics. These are hypotheses grounded in lived context — claims to test, not facts.

## Options and recommendation

**A — localise the Western playbook:** weak differentiation against funded incumbents. **B — own the multi-generational + help-coordination wedge:** start narrow on the sharpest validated pain where incumbents have no answer. **C — habit/behaviour-first:** design the whole product around the single-system habit rather than feature count. I'd lead with **B executed through a C lens** — pick the one coordination job incumbents ignore and obsess over making it a daily habit for the household's coordinator. Trade-off accepted: a narrow wedge sacrifices "all-in-one" breadth, but breadth is where I'd lose to Maple and Cozi.

## What I'd validate and measure

10–12 interviews across household structures, a week-long diary study to find the highest-friction recurring tasks, and — the strongest evidence — real activation and retention data from my own FamilyOS if the live product has users. North star: weekly active *households* where two or more members contributed. Risk: two-sided-within-the-home adoption — if only one member uses it, it fails.
